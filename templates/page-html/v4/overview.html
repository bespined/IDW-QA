Module Description

Before you can improve forensic practice, you need a clear-eyed model of how human performance actually works — and how it fails. This module gives you that foundation. Human factors is the study of how people interact with systems, tools, and environments — and forensic science is full of high-stakes moments where human cognitive limits directly affect outcomes.

We start with Signal Detection Theory, a framework developed in the 1950s that remains one of the most powerful tools for analyzing decisions made under uncertainty. You will see how forensic examiners — like any decision-maker — are always navigating a tension between sensitivity (catching real signals) and specificity (avoiding false alarms). Understanding that tension is step one in designing systems and standards that work with human cognition rather than against it.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this module, students will be able to:

  1. Define key concepts in human factors and explain their relevance to forensic science practice.
  2. Describe how cognitive and emotional biases influence decision-making in forensic contexts.
  3. Apply Signal Detection Theory (SDT) to forensic examples to explain types of decision errors and accuracy trade-offs.
  4. Identify and interpret professional or laboratory standards relevant to their forensic discipline.
  5. Analyze how specific provisions within these standards are designed to minimize human error or bias in forensic work.

Topics

In this module, we will cover:

  1. What is Human Factors?
  2. Human Performance and Error
  3. Cognitive and Emotional Biases in Forensic Decision-Making
  4. Signal Detection Theory (SDT): Framework for Understanding Decision Accuracy
  5. Interpreting and Applying Forensic Standards
  6. Linking Human Factors to Forensic Practice
