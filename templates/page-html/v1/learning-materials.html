Human Factors Foundations & Signal Detection Theory

Lectures, readings, and key concepts for the week

The big idea: Errors in forensic science are not random — they follow predictable patterns rooted in human cognitive architecture. Signal Detection Theory gives you a precise language for describing the trade-offs every forensic examiner makes, whether they realize it or not. This week's materials lay the groundwork for everything that follows.

🧠 Key Concepts

Core ideas from this module — use these to guide your reading and study.

A

The Human Factors Perspective

Human factors reframes error as a systems problem, not a personal failure. When a forensic examiner misidentifies a latent print, the question isn't "why did that examiner fail?" but "what conditions made that error possible — and how do we redesign them?"

Key terms: human factors, human performance, error taxonomy, systemic analysis, person-centered vs. system-centered blame

B

The Four SDT Outcomes

Every forensic decision produces one of four outcomes: a hit (correctly identifying a match), a miss (failing to identify a real match), a false alarm (incorrectly claiming a match), or a correct rejection (correctly finding no match). Understanding which type of error a standard is designed to prevent is essential to using it intelligently.

Key terms: hit rate, miss rate, false alarm rate, correct rejection, sensitivity (d'), criterion (β), ROC curve

C

Bias Is Not a Character Flaw

Cognitive biases — confirmation bias, contextual bias, anchoring — are not signs of incompetence. They are features of a cognitive system optimized for efficiency. The problem is that forensic work demands something that evolution didn't design for: unbiased evaluation of ambiguous physical evidence under institutional pressure. Standards and procedures exist to compensate for exactly this.

Key terms: confirmation bias, contextual bias, anchoring, availability heuristic, cognitive load, motivated reasoning

D

Reading Standards Through an SDT Lens

Every provision in a forensic quality standard is, at some level, a human factors intervention. Blind verification procedures reduce contextual bias. Proficiency testing reveals where sensitivity needs improvement. Documentation requirements create audit trails that expose criterion shifts. When you read a standard for Assignment 1, ask: what type of error is this provision trying to reduce?

Key terms: OSAC, proficiency testing, blind verification, accreditation, quality assurance, decision criterion
